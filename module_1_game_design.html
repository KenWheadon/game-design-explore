See It in Action

Here are three games that nail all 5 ingredients. Click through to see how:

Breakout / Brick Breaker

Goal: Destroy all the bricks before the ball falls past you
Rules: You control a paddle. The ball bounces on walls and bricks. If it hits the bottom, you lose.
Challenge: The ball gets faster, bricks are in different patterns, you have limited reaction time
Choices: Where do you position your paddle? Aggressive or defensive?
Feedback: Bricks vanish with a sound, score goes up, paddle lights up, ball changes color

Snake

Goal: Eat as much food as possible and get the highest score
Rules: You move in four directions. You can't reverse into yourself. The board wraps around (or has walls).
Challenge: As you eat, you grow longer and harder to control. The space gets cramped fast.
Choices: Which direction do you move? Risk the center or play it safe near edges?
Feedback: Your snake grows visibly, score updates, you hear a "pop" when you eat, game speeds up over time

Flappy Bird

Goal: Fly as far as possible without hitting pipes or the ground
Rules: You tap to make the bird fly up. Gravity pulls you down. Pipes move toward you constantly.
Challenge: One-pixel-perfect gaps. The timing is tight. One mistake and it's over.
Choices: When do you tap? Aim for the center or take risks?
Feedback: Score jumps up with a sound each time you pass a pipe, screen shakes, you hear a "thud" when you crash

Notice: All three games are simple. One core mechanic. But they all feel tight because all 5 ingredients work together.

What Actually Makes a Game Fun?

When you're starting out, focus on games with one or two of these DNA strands:

üéÅ Immediate Feedback & Progression ‚ñº

Games like clickers and idle games. You do something, you see the result instantly. Numbers go up. Every action has a visible consequence.

Example: Cookie Clicker. One click = instant feedback. It's addictive because it's satisfying.

‚öîÔ∏è Skill & Tension ‚ñº

Games like Flappy Bird, Snake, or Dodge. You're trying not to lose. There's a fail state that's always seconds away. You get better over time. This scratches the "mastery" itch.

Example: Flappy Bird. One wrong tap and you're done. The pressure keeps you engaged.

üéØ One Core Mechanic Done Really Well ‚ñº

Breakout. Pong. Tetris. These games aren't "about" depth‚Äîthey're about feeling good. When you hit the ball, it feels right. The controls respond instantly.

Example: Breakout. Hitting the ball with the paddle feels amazing because the timing is tight and the feedback is immediate.

Pro tip: Start with ONE of these. Don't try to build a story-rich RPG with 50 mechanics. Pick one thing and make it feel amazing.

Start 2D. Start Solo.

This isn't a limitation‚Äîit's a feature.

2D = Faster to Code & Easier to Polish

Single-Player = No Networking Headaches

Every game you see today started somewhere small. The developer didn't try to build their masterpiece on day one. They built something tight. Then they expanded.

Why 2D?

3D requires way more art, physics, and processing power. Your GPU has to work hard. Your artists have to model everything in three dimensions. Your code gets complicated fast.

2D lets you focus on what actually matters: making the core game loop feel good.

You can build, test, and refine a 2D game in hours instead of weeks. You can ship something that feels polished and tight, not bloated and half-finished.

Plus, on a Chromebook with a browser, 2D runs smoothly. 3D might choke.

Why Single-Player?

Multiplayer sounds fun, but it adds:

  • Servers (cost money, need maintenance)
  • Lag & latency issues (feels bad to play)
  • Matchmaking (complex code)
  • Debugging nightmares (two+ players = exponential complexity)

Single-player means you control the experience. You can focus on the game, not the infrastructure. You can make it feel amazing without worrying about a tenth of the complexity.

Once you nail single-player, then you can think about multiplayer.

From Idea to Playable

Here's how professionals actually make games:

1. Rough Concept ‚ñº

You have an idea. "I want a game where you dodge falling objects." That's it. One sentence. Don't overthink it.

2. The Core Loop ‚ñº

What's the one thing the player does over and over?

In dodge games: "Move left/right" ‚Üí "Avoid object" ‚Üí "Repeat."

Strip away everything else. The core loop is sacred.

3. Test & Refine ‚ñº

Build a playable version. Play it 50 times. Ask: "Is this fun?"

If yes, keep it. If no, figure out what's broken.

Maybe the difficulty curve is wrong. Maybe the controls feel sluggish. Maybe it's just boring. Fix it.

4. Polish & Expand ‚ñº

Once the core feels right, add layers: visual feedback, sounds, difficulty progression, menus.

But the game was already fun before you added any of that.

Real example: Flappy Bird was built in a few days. The core mechanic (tap to fly) was tested and felt right. Everything else just made it more polished. It wasn't complex‚Äîit was focused.

Bonus: What Separates Okay Games from Great Ones?

The fundamentals get you a working game. These details get you a game people want to play again.

‚úì The Details That Matter

Onboarding (Teaching Controls) ‚ñº

Never assume the player knows what to do. Show them in-game.

"Press SPACE to jump" or "Click to fire."

Make it impossible to fail at first. Let them learn before the real challenge starts.

Juice (Feedback & Polish) ‚ñº

This is the difference between a boring game and a satisfying one. When you hit something, does it feel like you hit it?

Add sounds, screen shake, particles, animations. Not bloat‚Äîjust feeling. Every action should have a reaction.

Clear Motivation ‚ñº

Why should the player care? What are they working toward? A high score? Survival? Helping a character?

Make it obvious. Even if it's just "beat your last score," the player needs to know what winning looks like.

Pacing (Start Easy, Ramp Up) ‚ñº

Don't throw your hardest challenge at the player immediately. Let them win at first. Build confidence.

Then slowly increase the difficulty. This keeps them engaged instead of frustrated.

Polish Over Scope ‚ñº

A polished 5-minute game beats an unfinished 2-hour game.

If you have two hours, use it to make one game feel incredible, not to build two mediocre games. Quality over quantity.
