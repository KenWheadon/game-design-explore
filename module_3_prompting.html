Pro Prompting Tips: Get Exactly What You Want

You've learned game design and the build process. Now learn how to ask for what you want.

The best games aren't built in one request. They're built through precision iteration:

1. Ask – Give the AI a clear, specific prompt
2. Receive – Get your playable game back
3. Test – Play it, find what's missing
4. Refine – Tell the AI exactly what to fix
5. Repeat – Usually 3-5 times per game

This module teaches you how to ask better. Better prompts = faster results = better games.

Start Every Prompt With These 4 Lines

Before you ask for anything, set the stage. Every single game prompt should start with these lines:

Create a complete HTML5 game with the following specifications: - The entire game must work in a single .HTML file - Use only vanilla JavaScript (no external libraries except for sounds/images) - Make it fun, playable, and polished from the start - Include a game-over state and a way to restart

Why these 4 lines?

Why These 4 Lines?

  • They prevent overthinking – The AI knows exactly what format you want
  • They force a complete game – You get a runnable file, not broken pieces
  • They set clear expectations – No external libraries = no dependencies, easy to share
  • They guarantee shareability – Single .HTML = can email it, upload it, paste it anywhere
  • They establish scope – The AI knows you want something polished and playable NOW, not a framework to build on

Tip 1: Be Ridiculously Specific

Vague prompts give vague results. Specific prompts give exactly what you want.

❌ Weak:
"Make a clicker game"
✓ Strong:
"Make a clicker game where you click a screaming avocado to collect guacamole. Each click earns 1 guacamole. Show a total guacamole counter at the top. When you reach 100 guacamole, show a 'WIN' screen."

Notice the difference:

  • The strong version has a theme (avocado + guacamole)
  • It specifies what happens (click = +1)
  • It defines when it ends (100 guacamole = win)

The weirder and more specific, the better. The AI's job is to execute your vision, not guess it.

Why does specificity matter?

Why Specificity Matters

  • Vague requests like "make it fun" mean different things – to you, to me, to the AI. Specific = no ambiguity
  • The AI doesn't know your visual style – Does "game" mean retro pixel art? Neon glow? Minimalist? You have to say
  • It cuts iteration time in half – You get closer to what you actually want on the first try
  • Weird themes are memorable – "Screaming avocado" is way more interesting than "generic clicker." Your game will stand out
  • The AI can execute what you describe – But it can't read your mind. Specificity = execution

Tip 2: Add Layers With Upgrades & Progression

A game where you just click is fun for 30 seconds. A game where you click to earn currency, then spend it on upgrades, is fun for 30 minutes.

Add this to any clicker or collection game:

Template:

"Include an upgrades shop that opens when the player presses U. Show 3 upgrades: [Upgrade 1], [Upgrade 2], [Upgrade 3]. Each upgrade costs [X currency] and provides [bonus effect]. Once bought, upgrades are permanent."

Example:

"Include an upgrades shop that opens when the player presses U. Show 3 upgrades: Double Click (costs 50 guac, doubles points per click), Auto Clicker (costs 200 guac, gains 1 guac per second), and Golden Avocado (costs 500 guac, triples all currency earned). Once bought, upgrades are permanent."

This simple addition creates a goal loop: click → earn → upgrade → click more → earn faster.

Why upgrades?

Why Upgrades Work

  • They create a meta-game – On top of the core game, there's now a goal loop
  • Players always have something to work toward – Not just clicking forever; they're saving for upgrades
  • It disguises grinding as progression – Clicking 500 times feels tedious. Saving for 3 upgrades feels intentional
  • It extends playtime without complexity – You didn't make the core mechanic harder; you just added layers
  • Upgrades feel rewarding – When a player buys an upgrade, they feel progression. Immediate feedback

Tip 3: Juice It Up (Particles, Sounds, Feedback)

A game that works is fine. A game that feels good is unforgettable.

Add this to any prompt:

Template:

"Add visual juice: particle effects when [action], screen shake when [event], and a satisfying sound effect for [interaction]. Make the [object] grow slightly when clicked and return to normal size."

Example:

"Add visual juice: particle effects (confetti or sparkles) when the avocado is clicked, screen shake (subtle, 100ms) when reaching a milestone (every 10 guacs), and a satisfying 'pop' sound for each click. Make the avocado grow 10% when clicked and return to normal size over 200ms."

These details cost nothing but transform the experience.

Why juice?

Why Juice Matters

  • Games with feedback feel more responsive – A click with feedback feels instant; a click with no response feels broken
  • Juice is the difference between prototype and polish – Same mechanics, different feel
  • It rewards player actions – When you click, something happens. Every sense gets feedback
  • Juice disguises simplicity as depth – A simple clicker with particles and sounds feels way more complex
  • It's free – Particle effects and sounds cost nothing to add and cost a lot to remove

Tip 4: Be Surgical With Fixes

You tested your game. Something's off. Don't ask for a rewrite. Be surgical.

❌ Instead of:
"The game feels wrong, fix it"
✓ Use:
"The avocado is too small and hard to click. Make it 50% bigger and make it glow when the player hovers over it."
❌ Instead of:
"Make it harder"
✓ Use:
"Spawning new avocados takes 3 seconds. Reduce it to 1.5 seconds. Also, after 30 clicks, increase the spawn rate by 20% every 10 clicks."

Surgical prompts:

  • Describe the problem in detail
  • Specify exactly what to change
  • Give numbers when possible (sizes, timing, colors)

Why be surgical?

Why Surgical Fixes Work

  • Broad requests risk breaking what already works – "Fix it" might make one thing better and break three things that were fine
  • Specific fixes keep your game stable – You're tweaking one aspect, not rewriting
  • Numbers prevent ambiguity – The AI knows exactly what you want (50% bigger, not "bigger")
  • This is how you iterate fast – Each loop is focused, not exploratory
  • You keep control – Every change is intentional. You're not asking the AI to decide what's wrong

Avoid These Beginner Mistakes

Some requests sound good but will waste your time. Learn what to avoid:

Don't Ask for 3D Games ▼

Why it's a trap: 3D requires way more code, assets, and processing power. Your game will be slow, bloated, and hard to polish.

Stick with 2D until you've shipped 10 games.

What to ask instead: "Make a top-down 2D game with a bird's-eye camera view" or "Make a side-scrolling 2D platformer"

Don't Ask for Multiplayer ▼

Why it's a trap: Multiplayer requires servers, networking, matchmaking. It's 10x more complex than single-player. You can't test it easily locally.

Keep it single-player for your first 20 games.

What to ask instead: "Make this a single-player game with leaderboard tracking (local storage only)" or "Add a two-player mode that plays on the same keyboard locally"

Don't Ask for Scope Creep ▼

Why it's a trap: Asking for everything at once gives you nothing. A broken mess, not a game.

Example of scope creep: "Make a full RPG with story, inventory, crafting, NPCs, and combat" — This won't work in one prompt.

What to ask instead: Start with one core mechanic: "Make a text-based dungeon crawler where I choose left/right at each turn and fight one type of enemy" — then iterate and expand from there.

Don't Ask for External APIs ▼

Why it's a trap: Your single .HTML file can't reliably pull data from the internet. APIs require authentication, handling errors, CORS, and backend setup.

Keep it local. A single .HTML file can't do backend stuff.

What to ask instead: "Use in-game data only" or "Generate random data locally" or "Let the player input their own data"

Don't Assume the AI Knows Your Vision ▼

Why it's a trap: You're imagining a specific game in your head. The AI probably doesn't know that exact game.

Example: "Make it look like [game you're thinking of]" — The AI might not know that game, or might interpret it differently.

What to ask instead: Describe it: "Make it look retro and pixelated, like 8-bit NES games. Use a 4-color palette: black, white, red, yellow."
